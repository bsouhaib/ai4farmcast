2. Why is forecasting so hard?

Weather may seem predictable at times, but the atmosphere is a chaotic system - where even the smallest change can lead to dramatically different outcomes. Forecasting is further challenged by data gaps, measurement errors, and the sheer complexity of processes like cloud formation and land-atmosphere interactions. And as forecasts look further ahead, their accuracy naturally declines, making long-range predictions especially difficult. For more details, watch the video below.

3. Forecasting across timescales

Weather forecasts operate on different timescales - from nowcasting that focuses on the next few hours, to short-range, medium-range, subseasonal (S2S), and even seasonal outlooks. But no matter the timescale, one thing remains true: forecast confidence often declines the further ahead we look.

5. Historical evolution of forecasting

From the early days of weather prediction - built on observations and pattern recognition - to the rise of Numerical Weather Prediction (NWP), forecasting has continually evolved. Ensemble forecasting brought a way to account for uncertainty, and now AI models represent the latest step forward, working alongside physics-based approaches to push forecasting capabilities even further.

6. Some basics of AI for weather forecasting

AI brings a new dimension to weather forecasting by learning patterns from large datasets. Rather than replacing physics-based models, it works alongside them - offering strengths in fast updates, downscaling, and pattern recognition. Like any tool, its value comes with limits, it works best when supported by high-quality data, backed by rigorous validation, and guided by careful interpretation.
